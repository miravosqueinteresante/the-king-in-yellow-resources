# Lovecraft and The King in Yellow

H.P. Lovecraft discovered Robert W. Chambers' *The King in Yellow* early in his career and was deeply influenced by its atmosphere of cosmic dread and its technique of suggesting horror through allusion rather than direct description. While Lovecraft's own mythos would later overshadow Chambers' work, he freely acknowledged his debt.

## Key Borrowings

### Hastur

In Chambers, Hastur is mentioned only in passing — it appears as a place name or perhaps a deity in the forbidden play "The King in Yellow." Lovecraft seized on this name and, with the help of August Derleth, transformed Hastur into one of the Great Old Ones of the Cthulhu Mythos. In Lovecraft's "The Whisperer in Darkness" (1931), Hastur is referenced as an entity of immense power, associated with the Hyades star cluster and the Yellow Sign. Derleth later codified Hastur as the "Prince of the Elements" and "The Unspeakable One," often linked to the element of air and the power of the wind.

### The Yellow Sign

Chambers' Yellow Sign — a strange, hieroglyphic symbol that drives its beholders toward madness — became in Lovecraft's cosmos a tangible link between our world and the outer horrors. While Lovecraft never described the Sign in detail, Derleth and the Chaosium roleplaying tradition would later specify it as a triskelion-like glyph of three bent arms, reminiscent of the Hyades star cluster.

### Carcosa and the Lake of Hali

The ruined city of Carcosa and its black, still lake appear in Chambers as shadowy concepts from the play within the book. Lovecraft absorbed these images into his own geography of cosmic horror, and later writers in the Cthulhu Mythos tradition placed Carcosa in the Hyades as a physical (or extra-dimensional) location reachable through forbidden means.

## The Call of Cthulhu (1928)

While "The Call of Cthulhu" does not directly mention Chambers, its structure — a forbidden text, a hidden cult, a symbol that drives men mad — owes much to the technique Chambers pioneered. The narrator's gradual discovery of the Cthulhu cult, pieced together from scattered documents and testimonies, mirrors the way Chambers' characters gradually uncover the truth about the Yellow Sign.

Here is a key passage that captures the cosmic dread both authors shared:

> "The most merciful thing in the world, I think, is the inability of the human mind to correlate all its contents. We live on a placid island of ignorance in the midst of black seas of infinity, and it was not meant that we should voyage far."

## The Whisperer in Darkness (1931)

This is Lovecraft's most direct engagement with Chambers' mythos. The story features:

- **The Mi-Go** — extraterrestrial fungoid beings who communicate through a strange buzzing or whispering, reminiscent of the unearthly sounds in Chambers' "The Yellow Sign"
- **Hastur** — explicitly mentioned alongside Yog-Sothoth and other entities
- **The Hyades** — the star cluster that Lovecraft linked to Hastur and the Yellow Sign, building on Chambers' astronomical references
- **Cosmic indifference** — the theme that humanity is insignificant before vast, unknowable forces, which Chambers first explored in his play-within-a-book

The story is told through a series of letters and documents, a technique Lovecraft perfected and which echoes Chambers' framing device of the forbidden play.

## Later Developments

After Lovecraft's death, August Derleth systematized the Cthulhu Mythos and explicitly connected Chambers' creations to Lovecraft's pantheon:

| Element | In Chambers | In the Cthulhu Mythos |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| Hastur | A name or place in the play | A Great Old One, half-brother to Cthulhu |
| The Yellow Sign | A symbol of forbidden knowledge | An artifact that summons Hastur |
| Carcosa | A lost city in an unknown world | A city in the Hyades or a dream-world |
| The King in Yellow | The title of the forbidden play | An avatar or aspect of Hastur |
| Lake of Hali | A black lake in Carcosa | The boundary between worlds |

## Conclusion

Lovecraft's transformation of Chambers' delicate, suggestive horrors into a systematized cosmic mythos represents one of the most fascinating literary appropriations in weird fiction. While Chambers moved on to write romance novels, his brief foray into cosmic horror — filtered through Lovecraft's imagination — became the foundation of an entire genre.
